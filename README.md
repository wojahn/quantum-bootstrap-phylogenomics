Non-parametric phylogenetic bootstrapping and topological ensemble cloud generation leveraging discrete quantum optimization architectures and QUBO mapping frameworks.

# Summary
Statistical branch support is a critical prerequisite for interpreting phylogenetic trees, yet existing quantum phylogenetic frameworks produce no confidence metrics for reconstructed topologies. We present a hybrid pipeline that addresses this gap by embedding a quantum-inspired, top-down graph-partitioning engine within a classical nonparametric bootstrap loop, enabling the direct calculation of Quantum Bootstrap Support (QBS) values. Applied to 19 Pandanaceae plastomes as a proof-of-concept, the pipeline recovered topologies congruent with established Maximum Likelihood (ML) reference trees across 100 independent simulated annealing replicates. QBS values were substantially lower than both classical ultrafast bootstrap scores (median decrease: −42.5 ± 49.5%) and Classical Distance Bootstrap Support (CDBS) values generated by Neighbor-Joining on identical input matrices (median decrease: −38 ± 50%). Because CDBS on the same cophenetic matrices achieved near-perfect support (median: 100%), QBS depression is directly attributable to the annealing optimizer rather than to distance-matrix compression as previously hypothesized. Robinson-Foulds distances across QBS replicates nonetheless formed a tight, non-random distribution (median: 14.0; Normalized RF: 0.44), well below random expectation, revealing a dissociation between topological fidelity and nodal confidence in the annealing framework. These results establish a statistically rigorous, reproducible framework for integrating quantum-inspired architectures into molecular systematics and identify improvement of the QUBO objective function and annealing schedule as the most tractable priorities for next-generation quantum phylogenetic methods.
 
# Introduction
Bootstrapping assesses estimator variance by iteratively resampling, with replacement, N individuals from an original dataset of size N and calculating the statistic of interest across pseudo-replicates (Efron, 1979). This approach is asymptotically more accurate than single-sample inference under normality assumptions (DiCiccio and Efron, 1996). Felsenstein (1985) first applied this framework to phylogenetics, using nonparametric resamples to generate node support values for evolutionary trees, a necessity given that the number of possible topologies grows exponentially with taxon number (Felsenstein, 1978), placing full enumeration of treespace beyond the reach of parametric methods. Mechanistically, taxa sharing many character states co-occur across bootstrap replicates at high frequency, while those sharing few do not, directly linking genomic signal density to statistical confidence.
Nonparametric bootstrapping is conventionally paired with four tree-reconstruction paradigms: Maximum Parsimony, which minimizes total evolutionary changes across resampled alignment columns (Felsenstein, 1985); Neighbor-Joining, a greedy bottom-up clustering algorithm operating on pairwise distance matrices (Saitou and Nei, 1987); Minimum Evolution, which selects the topology minimizing total branch length (Rzhetsky and Nei, 1992); and Maximum Likelihood, which evaluates the probability that a substitution model would generate the observed data across competing topologies (Felsenstein, 1981, 1985).
Until recently, all such frameworks have been executed on classical hardware, where computation proceeds through deterministic bit-state logic gates yielding a single output per instruction. Quantum computers instead operate on qubits, engineered from quantum systems such as photons or electrons, which exploit superposition to represent multiple states simultaneously (Nielsen and Chuang, 2010; Dirac, 1958; de Ronde, 2015), and quantum entanglement (Einstein, 1935) and constructive interference (Deutsch, 1985) to navigate complex optimization landscapes more efficiently than classical heuristics. Quantum annealing (Kadowaki and Nishimori, 1998) maps a combinatorial problem directly onto a qubit lattice (Lucas, 2014) and allows the system to relax to its lowest energy state, which corresponds to the optimal solution.
Fernández-Otero et al. (2026) recently applied this architecture to phylogenetic inference, replacing traditional bottom-up clustering with a recursive, top-down graph-partitioning framework governed by the Normalized Cut (Ncut) criterion, formulated as a Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) model executable via simulated quantum annealing or the Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm (QAOA). Their benchmarking against Neighbor-Joining revealed a substantial performance gap, approximately 40% lower topology recovery on average, which they attributed to the NMcut method rather than quantum hardware error, and which was similarly documented on the quantum-inspired Digital Annealer by Onodera et al. (2023). Critically, however, neither study could isolate the optimizer as the sole source of this gap, as their NJ and quantum comparators operated on different input representations. Furthermore, both studies evaluated single-tree topology recovery without bootstrap resampling, leaving reconstructed topologies without any measure of statistical confidence.
Our pipeline addresses both limitations. First, we embed a top-down quantum graph-partitioning engine within a classical nonparametric bootstrap loop (Fig. 1), enabling direct calculation of QBS values, the first framework to couple frequentist resampling with a quantum-inspired phylogenetic optimizer. Second, we introduce a Classical Distance Bootstrap Support (CDBS) comparator that runs Neighbor-Joining on the identical cophenetic matrices used as quantum annealing input, isolating the optimizer as the sole variable and providing the controlled contrast absent from prior benchmarking. Classical resampling serves as the outer loop while the annealing engine handles tree reconstruction within each replicate, making the pipeline immediately executable on software-based simulators without physical quantum hardware. As a proof-of-concept, we apply this hybrid framework to 19 Pandanaceae plastomes and validate performance through four complementary approaches: mapping quantum-derived topologies onto a fixed ML reference backbone to calculate QBS values; comparing QBS against CDBS on identical inputs to isolate optimizer behavior; compiling the 100 replicate topologies into a DensiTree cloudscape to visualize topological variance; and calculating RF distances across the distribution to test whether the annealing engine converges on a biologically plausible, constrained neighborhood of treespace.

# Methods
## Taxon Sampling
Nineteen Pandanaceae plastomes were selected for analysis (Table 1), building on the evolutionary frameworks of Wojahn et al. (2025, 2026). The ingroup comprises 16 Pandanus taxa representing four clades at sampling depths roughly proportional to species richness (Clade Ia: 3; Ib: 5; IIa: 3; IIb: 5), with two Benstonea outgroups and Freycinetia baueriana as the designated root. This dataset was chosen because: (1) phylogenetic relationships are well-characterized in both maternal and biparental lineages (Buerki et al., 2012; Gallaher et al., 2016; Wojahn et al., 2025, 2026); (2) whole plastomes provide sufficient variation for phylogenetic inference while remaining computationally tractable for quantum simulation architectures; and (3) the non-recombining nature of plastomes eliminates intra-genomic gene tree conflict.

## Sequence Preparation and Alignment
The IRB inverted repeat was removed from each plastome to prevent artificial overrepresentation of duplicate characters, and only plastomes with ≥98% whole-genome coverage were retained. All 19 sequences were analyzed as a single unpartitioned matrix. Multi-locus partitioning, employed in prior frameworks (Wojahn et al., 2025, 2026), was not applied here because separate parameter spaces disrupt the uniform network mapping required for QUBO problem formulation; treating the plastome as a single evolutionary locus preserves distance matrix integrity across the annealing pipeline. Sequences were processed in R v4.4.0 using the Biostrings (Pagès et al., 2024) and DECIPHER packages. Profile-to-profile alignment was performed with a guide tree via DECIPHER::AlignSeqs (Wright, 2016) with multithreaded scaling, followed by gap smoothing with DECIPHER::AdjustAlignment. Alignment integrity was verified manually in SeaView v5.0 (Gouy et al., 2010).

## Maximum Likelihood Reference Tree
ML inference and substitution model selection were performed on the master alignment in IQ-TREE 2 v2.3.5 (Minh et al., 2020) using ModelFinder Plus (Kalyaanamoorthy et al., 2017; Nguyen et al., 2015). The optimal topology was inferred under the best-fit model with a continuous Gamma distribution and 1,000 UFBoot replicates (Hoang et al., 2018). The resulting tree was rooted on F. baueriana and ladderized in ape (Paradis and Schliep, 2019) to serve as the comparative reference backbone.

## Bootstrap Resampling and Tree Optimization
The master alignment was converted to a phyDat matrix in ape v5.0 (Paradis et al., 2004) and phangorn v2.12.1 (Schliep, 2011). One hundred non-parametric bootstrap replicates were generated via phangorn::bootstrap.phyDat (Felsenstein, 1985). For each replicate, a starting topology was estimated from a Jukes-Cantor distance matrix using Neighbor-Joining, then fully optimized under GTR+Γ with Nearest-Neighbor Interchange rearrangements via phangorn::optim.pml. Each optimized tree was converted to a cophenetic distance matrix and exported as a structured CSV file. These 100 cophenetic matrices served as the shared input for both the quantum annealing and classical distance bootstrap pipelines described below, ensuring that any performance differences between the two are attributable solely to the tree-building engine rather than to input data.

## Classical Distance Bootstrap Support
To provide a controlled comparator isolating the quantum annealing optimizer as the sole variable, NJ trees were reconstructed directly from each of the 100 cophenetic matrices using ape::nj. Bipartition frequencies from these 100 NJ topologies were mapped onto the ML reference backbone via phangorn::plotBS to calculate Classical Distance Bootstrap Support (CDBS) values. RF distances between the ML backbone and each NJ replicate were calculated using the TreeDist package (Smith, 2020). This design differs critically from the NJ comparisons of Fernández-Otero et al. (2026) and Onodera et al. (2023), in which NJ and quantum methods operated on different input representations; here, both pipelines consume identical matrices, enabling direct attribution of any support differences to the optimizer.

## QUBO Mapping
The 100 cophenetic matrices were ingested into a Python 3.11 (Van Rossum and Drake, 2022) environment via the reticulate interface (Ushey et al., 2024). Each matrix was transformed into an edge-weighted similarity graph by subtracting pairwise distances from the observed maximum, with self-loops nullified along the diagonal. Phylogenetic reconstruction was framed as a top-down recursive hierarchical graph partitioning problem under the Normalized Minimum Cut criterion (Shi and Malik, 2000; Onodera et al., 2023), mapped into QUBO form using the PhyloTree_ReConst API (Fernández-Otero et al., 2026), producing a Q-matrix compatible with Ising-model annealing architectures.

## Simulated Quantum Annealing
QUBO problems were solved using the simulation suite of the D-Wave Ocean SDK (D-Wave Systems, 2021), configured to mirror the minimization logic of a physical Pegasus qubit topology (Boothby et al., 2020) without hardware constraints. Each of the 100 bootstrap matrices was processed with 1,000 relaxation steps per graph-cut calculation under an optimized cooling schedule targeting the global minimum energy state at each bipartition.

## Topological Processing and Support Mapping
Solved tree structures were parsed into Newick strings using the PhyloTree_ReConst labels-aware parser (Ferro, 2024) and written to a master file (quantum_bootstrap_trees.tre). QBS values were calculated by mapping bipartition frequencies from the 100 annealing topologies onto the ML reference backbone using phangorn::plotBS (Schliep, 2011). Freycinetia baueriana was subsequently dropped to isolate the 18-taxon ingroup for visualization.

## Topological Variance and Nodal Depth Analysis
Absolute RF distances (Robinson and Foulds, 1981) between the ML backbone and each bootstrap replicate were calculated using the TreeDist package (Smith, 2020). Tree resolution was monitored by computing the resolution percentage across all 100 replicates in ape. For nodes with QBS < 70%, normalized root-to-tip depths were calculated via ape::node.depth.edgelength on a scale from 0.0 (root) to 1.0 (furthest tip), and the median relative depth was used to characterize the spatial distribution of topological conflict. All figures, including the QBS-annotated tree, DensiTree cloudscape (Schliep et al., 2015), and RF distance histogram, were rendered in ape and phangorn.

# Results
## Classical Maximum Likelihood Phylogenetic Inference
The ML topology recovered here is congruent with those reported in Wojahn et al. (2025, 2026), demonstrating that the reduced taxon sampling employed in this study does not produce aberrant topological results relative to more comprehensively sampled analyses (Fig. 2a). Nearly all nodes were fully resolved, with a median UFBoot score of 100%.

## Classical Distance Bootstrap Support
NJ reconstruction from the 100 cophenetic matrices yielded CDBS values that were near-perfect across all internal nodes (median: 100%; minimum: 87%; maximum: 100%), with a median RF distance of 0.0 against the ML reference backbone. The tight RF distribution and high nodal support confirm that the GTR+Γ-optimized cophenetic matrices carry sufficient phylogenetic signal to recover the ML topology reliably under classical distance-based reconstruction. These results establish a performance ceiling for the shared input data and provide the controlled baseline against which QBS values are interpreted.

## Quantum Bootstrap Support
Mapping 100 synchronized bootstrap topologies onto the ML backbone yielded QBS scores spanning a broad range, from a minimum of 4% to a maximum of 95%, with a median of 57.3%, substantially lower than both their UFBoot counterparts (median decrease: −42.5 ± 49.5%) and their CDBS counterparts on identical input matrices (median decrease: −38 ± 50%). Because CDBS on the same cophenetic matrices achieves near-perfect support, the QBS depression cannot be attributed to distance-matrix compression and is instead directly attributable to the annealing optimizer. The high standard deviation in both delta distributions indicates that support depression was not uniform across nodes, implicating a localized rather than globally acting phenomenon (Fig. 2b).

## Topological Divergence
RF distances for the QBS replicates formed a tight, non-random distribution (minimum: 8.0; median: 14.0; maximum: 24.0), contrasting sharply with the CDBS median RF of 0.0. Normalized against the maximum theoretical symmetric difference for a 19-taxon tree (32.0), the QBS median RF corresponds to a Normalized RF of 0.44. The maximum observed divergence (24.0) remained well below both the theoretical maximum and the expected value under random topology sampling, confirming that the annealing engine explored a constrained, biologically meaningful neighborhood of treespace rather than sampling stochastically (Figs. 2a/c). Together, the CDBS and QBS RF profiles reveal a dissociation between topological fidelity and nodal confidence in the annealing framework: the optimizer reliably recovers gross topological structure but introduces uncertainty at finer hierarchical levels.

## Spatial Distribution of Nodal Conflict
Of all internal nodes, 12 fell below the 70% QBS threshold. Normalized root-to-tip depth of these weak nodes (0.0 = root; 1.0 = tips) revealed a median relative depth of 0.64, indicating that low-support conflict was concentrated in the mid-phylogeny rather than at either the deep backbone or the shallow terminal divergences (Fig. 2b). This spatial pattern is consistent with the node-by-node QBS vs. CDBS delta distribution, in which the high standard deviation (±50%) reflects node-specific rather than global support depression, and suggests the annealing engine encounters its greatest difficulty at intermediate-depth partitioning decisions where graph similarity structure is most ambiguous.

# Discussion
This study evaluates a novel quantum-inspired optimization framework applied to a plastome dataset of 19 terminal taxa within Pandanaceae. The pipeline demonstrated strong algorithmic stability, recovering congruent macro-topological structures across independent bootstrap replicates within a simulated quantum annealing environment (Fig. 2c). Validation against a Maximum Likelihood (ML) reference tree revealed that Quantum Bootstrap Support (QBS) values are characteristically more conservative than classical ultrafast bootstrap values (Figs. 2a/b). A controlled Classical Distance Bootstrap Support (CDBS) comparator further demonstrates that this depression is attributable to the annealing optimizer rather than to distance-matrix compression. Nevertheless, a median Robinson-Foulds (RF) distance of 14.0 (Normalized RF = 0.44) confirms that the graph-partitioning engine converges on biologically plausible evolutionary trajectories rather than sampling unconstrained tree space.

## Interpretation of QBS Depression Relative to UFBoot
Support depression in QBS values, concentrated along intermediate internal nodes (Fig. 2b), could in principle reflect either genuine phylogenetic uncertainty captured by quantum-inspired computation but masked in the ML tree, or an information-flattening artifact inherent to distance-matrix compression. The consistency of inferred topologies across independent bootstrap replicates (Figs. 2c/d) argues against systemic instability as the cause. Because this pipeline operates on pairwise distance matrices rather than discrete character states, distance-matrix compression remained a plausible explanation for support depression, however, the CDBS comparator described below resolves this ambiguity directly.

## Classical Distance Bootstrap Support as a Controlled Comparator
To isolate the contribution of the quantum annealing optimizer to observed support depression, we introduced a CDBS comparator by running NJ directly on the same 100 GTR+Γ-optimized cophenetic matrices used as input to the simulated annealing pipeline. This design provides a controlled contrast absent from prior work: Fernández-Otero et al. (2026) compared their QNMcut method against NJ, but used different input matrices for each, their NJ operated on the raw similarity matrix while QNMcut operated on the QUBO-mapped formulation, simultaneously confounding input type and optimizer as sources of performance divergence. Similarly, Onodera et al. (2023) benchmarked NMcutDA against NJ and ML on single-tree topology recovery without bootstrap resampling, using the percentage of correctly recovered branches as the sole metric. Neither study isolated the optimizer as the sole variable. By holding input matrices constant across both pipelines, our CDBS comparator achieves this isolation for the first time.
The results are unambiguous. CDBS achieved near-perfect support across all nodes (median: 100%; minimum: 87%), with a median RF distance of 0.0 against the ML reference backbone, while QBS on identical inputs yielded a median support of 57.3% and a median RF distance of 14.0, a per-node median delta of −38 ± 50%. Because the only variable separating these two pipelines is the tree-building engine, this difference is directly attributable to the annealing optimizer rather than to distance-matrix compression. This finding both extends and sharpens the conclusion of Fernández-Otero et al. (2026), who attributed the quantum performance gap to the NMcut method rather than quantum hardware error, by demonstrating that the same performance gap manifests in the bootstrap support domain under controlled input conditions. Notably, the high standard deviation of the delta (±50%) mirrors the node-depth audit finding that support depression is concentrated at mid-phylogeny nodes rather than distributed uniformly, suggesting the annealing engine struggles specifically with intermediate-depth partitioning decisions where graph similarity structure is most ambiguous, rather than failing globally.

## Validation of a Non-Random Search
Despite the support depression identified above, the tight RF distance distribution across bootstrap replicates demonstrates that the quantum-inspired backend executes a structured, guided search rather than stochastic sampling. That QBS recovers gross topological structure consistently, with observed RF distances well below random expectation, while simultaneously underperforming NJ on branch support indicates a dissociation between topological fidelity and nodal confidence in the annealing framework. The optimizer reliably identifies the correct large-scale partitions of treespace but introduces uncertainty at finer hierarchical levels, particularly at mid-phylogeny depths. This pattern is consistent with the known behavior of simulated annealing on graph-cut problems: global energy minimization is robust, but local partition boundaries near the decision threshold are sensitive to stochastic relaxation dynamics (Figs. 2a/c).

## Technical Limitations
Several constraints warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, the CDBS results demonstrate that the annealing optimizer, rather than distance-matrix input, is the primary source of support depression, implying that improvements to the QUBO formulation or annealing schedule, rather than input data type, are the most productive targets for future development. Second, reliance on distance matrices nonetheless precludes partitioned models, which may bias inferences from multi-gene or phylogenomic datasets where rate heterogeneity among loci is substantial. Third, the pipeline produces strictly unscaled cladograms, lacking branch-length information required for molecular dating or ancestral trait reconstruction. Fourth, computational scaling on classical hardware remains a practical ceiling: the 19-taxon proof-of-concept presented here will require physical quantum hardware acceleration as dataset size increases.

## Future Directions
As quantum hardware and digital annealing platforms mature, the limitations identified here are expected to diminish in parallel. Future implementations on physical quantum processors or advanced annealers will enable processing of substantially larger, multi-gene datasets, transitioning quantum phylogenetics from proof-of-concept to high-throughput application. Given that the CDBS comparator localizes performance depression to the optimizer, the most tractable near-term improvement path lies in refining the QUBO objective function and annealing schedule rather than changing the input data architecture, particularly targeting the intermediate-depth partition decisions where stochastic relaxation appears most disruptive. More broadly, a hybrid quantum-classical architecture remains the most viable framework: classical processors handle alignment and distance calculation, while quantum processors resolve topologically intractable regions of treespace. Finally, the DensiTree visualizations produced here suggest that the intrinsic behavior of quantum annealing, naturally relaxing into distributions of low-energy ground states, may be uniquely suited to representing genuine evolutionary ambiguity, offering a principled framework for visualizing clouds of alternative histories rather than forcing convergence on a single optimal topology.
